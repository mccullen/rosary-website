{{ kindsOfPrayer.text }}

The Rosary consists of two kinds of prayer: vocal prayer and mental prayer.

{{ vocalPrayers.text }}

The vocal prayers of the Rosary consists in saying fifteen or twenty decades of the Hail Mary, each headed by an Our Father and followed by a Glory Be to the Father and the optional Fatima prayer.

See the first Rose of The Admirable Secret of the Rosary

{{ mentalPrayers.text }}

The mental prayers of the Rosary consists in meditating upon and contemplating the chief mysteries of the life, death, and glory of Jesus Christ and His Blessed Mother at the same time the vocal prayers are being recited.

See the first Rose of The Admirable Secret of the Rosary

{{ fruitOfTheMysteries.text }}

The fruits of the Mysteries of the Rosary are the principle virtues which Jesus and Mary practiced and we seek to imitate.

See the first Rose of The Admirable Secret of the Rosary

{{ seriesOfTheRosary.text }}

The Mysteries of the Rosary are grouped into three or four series of five decades each. Each series covers a part of Christ's life. The (optional) Mysteries of Light series was established by St. John Paul II in his ensyclical "On the Rosary":

  1. Joyful Mysteries: Mysteries of Jesus's early life and the incarnation
  2. Mysteries of Light (optional): Mysteries of Jesus's later life and ministry
  3. Sorrowful Mysteries: Mysteries of Jesus's passion and death
  4. Glorious Mysteries: Mysteries of Jesus's resurrection and glory

The origin of the Rosary is the Prayer of Christ and the Angelic Salutation. It was the first prayer and devotion of the faithful and has been in use from the time of the Apostles and disciples down to the present. But it has evolved over the centuries and taken upon a more mature form.

See the second Rose of The Admirable Secret of the Rosary

1214: The Church received the Rosary in its present form after St. Dominic received it from the Blessed Virgin to convert the Albigensian heretics and other sinners. (St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Second Rose)

At this time, it was called the Psalter of Jesus and Mary because it had the same number of Angelic Salutations as there are psalms in the Book of the Psalms of David (150). Since simple and uneducated people could not say David’s Psalter, they would say the Psalter of Jesus and Mary instead (St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary , Sixth Rose).

1460: The Rosary, having lost its first fervor after a century, is restored by Blessed Alan de la Roche’s preaching after Our Lord spoke to him in the Sacred Host and reproached him for not preaching the Rosary despite his learning and understanding (St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Fourth Rose).

2002: St. Pope John Paul II introduced the “Mysteries of Light” (the Luminous Mysteries) as an optional series of mysteries in his Apostolic Letter on the Rosary, Rosarium Virginis Mariae, and provides guidance on how to better pray the Rosary.

The story is recorded in the book De Dignitate Psalterii by Blessed Alan de la Roche. St. Dominic saw that the gravity of people's sins was hindering the conversion of the Albigensians so he withdrew into a forest near Toulouse and prayed unceasingly for three days and three nights. He did nothing but weep and do harsh penances to appease the anger of Almighty God. He disciplined his body so much that his body was lacerated and he finally fell into a coma.

Then Our Lady appeared and told him that the battering ram in this kind of warfare has always been the Angelic Psalter, the foundation stone of the New Testament. So she told him he must preach her Psalter to reach hardened souls and win them over to God. He went to the Cathedral and at once unseen Angels rang the bells to gather the people together so he could preach.

At the beginning of his sermon an appalling storm broke out and shook the earth. The sun was darkened and there was so much thunder and lightening that all we very much afraid. There was a picture of Our Lady exposed in a prominent place and they saw her raise her arms to Heaven three times to call down God's vegeance upon them if they failed to be converted, amend their lives, and seek the protection of the Holy Mother of God.

The storm came to an end at the prayer of St. Dominic. He fervently and compellingly explained the importance and value of the Holy Rosary so almost all the people of Toulouse embraced it and renounced their false beliefs and began leading Christian lives, giving up their former bad habits.

Inspired by the Holy Spirit, instructed by the Blessed Virgin as well as by his own experience, Saint Dominic preached the Rosary for the rest of his life. He preached it by his example as well as by his sermons, in cities and in country places, to people of high station and low, before scholars and the uneducated, to Catholics and to heretics. The Rosary was called the Psalter of Jesus and Mary because it has the same number of Angelic Salutations as there are psalms in the Book of the Psalms of David. Since simple and uneducated people were not able to say the Psalms of David the Rosary is held to be just as fruitful for them.

  1. It bears the fruit of the Word Incarnate whereas David’s Psalter only prophecies His coming.
  2. The fruit of the real thing is more important that its prefiguration
  3. It is the direct work of the Most Blessed Trinity and was not made through a human instrument.

The Psalter or Rosary of our Lady is divided into three chaplets of five decades each, for the following reasons:

  1. to honour the three persons of the Blessed Trinity
  2. to honour the life, death and glory of Jesus Christ
  3. to imitate the Church triumphant, to help the members of the Church militant, and to bring relief to the Church suffering
  4. to imitate the three groups into which the psalms are divided, the first being for the purgative life, the second for the illuminative life, and the third for the unitive life
  5. to give us graces in abundance during life, peace at death, and glory in eternity
St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Sixth Rose

The name “Rosary” means “Crown of Roses” (St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Seventh Rose)

The Rosary is a Crown of Roses because "every time people say the Rosary devoutly they place on the heads of Jesus and Mary 153 white roses and sixteen red roses. Being heavenly flowers, these roses will never fade or lose their beauty." (St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Seventh Rose)

The Rosary is symbolized as being of heavenly flowers. That is, roses that will never fade or lose their exquisite beauty as do temporal things of this world. The prayers are represented by different roses

  • Hail Mary: White Rose
  • Our Father: Red Rose
  • Rosary of Five Decades: little wreath of flowers or small crown of heavenly roses
  • Entire Rosary: large Crown of Roses

He said his Rosary with such fervor that he often saw a red rose come out of his mouth at each Our Father and a white rose at each Hail Mary.

The chronicles of St. Francis tell of a young friar who had the praiseworthy habit of saying this crown of our Lady every day before dinner. One day, for some reason or other, he did not manage to say it. The refectory bell had already been rung when he asked the Superior to allow him to say it before coming to the table, and, having obtained permission, he withdrew to his cell to pray.

After he had been gone a long time, the Superior sent another friar to fetch him, and he found him in his room bathed in a heavenly light in the presence of our Lady and two angels. Beautiful roses kept issuing from his mouth at each Hail Mary, and the two angels were taking them one by one and placing them on our Lady's head, while she smilingly accepted them. Finally, two other friars who had been sent to find out what had happened to the first two saw the same scene, and our Lady did not leave until the whole Rosary had been said.

St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Seventh Rose

Alphonsus, King of Leon and Galicia, very much wanted all his servants to honour the Blessed Virgin by saying the Rosary, so he used to hang a large rosary on his belt, though he never said it himself. Nevertheless, his wearing it encouraged his courtiers to say the Rosary devoutly.

One day the King fell seriously ill and when he was given up for dead he found himself, in spirit, before the judgment-seat of our Lord. Many devils were there accusing him of all the sins he had committed, and our Lord was about to condemn him when our Lady came forward to speak in his favour. She called for a pair of scales and had his sins placed in one of the balances, while she put the large rosary which he had always worn on the other scale, together with all the rosaries that had been said through his example. It was found that the Rosaries weighed more than his sins.

Looking at him with great kindness, our Lady said, "As a reward for the little service you did for me in wearing my rosary, I have obtained a great grace for you from my Son. Your life will be spared for a few more years. See that you spend those years wisely, and do penance."

When the King regained consciousness he cried out, "Blessed be the Rosary of the most holy Virgin Mary, by which I have been de-livered from eternal damnation."

After he had recovered his health, he spent the rest of his life in spreading devotion to the Rosary, and said it faithfully every day.

St. Louis-Marie de Montfort, The Admirable Secret of the Rosary, Eighth Rose

{{ essenceOfRosary.text }}

The Rosary is essentially composed of an introduction followed by twenty “mysteries” (significant events or moments in the life of Jesus and Mary) which are contemplated over the vocal prayers

  1. Vocal Prayers
    • Hail Mary
    • Our Father
    • Glory Be to the Father
  2. Meditations on the Gospel (The good news of our salvation through the life, ministry, passion, and resurrection of Jesus)
